Safeguarding Adults (Prevention of Abuse) Policy

This policy has been drafted with reference to The Care Act 2014, Protection of Freedoms Act 2012 (Part 5), Surrey Multi Agency Safeguarding Hub (MASH), Surrey Safeguarding Adults Board (SSAB), The Association of Directors of Social Services (ADASS) and the Deaprtment of Health and Social Care's Care and Support Statutory Guidance.

Birtley House recognises the six guiding principles of safeguarding:

  1. Empowerment – residents are supported in making their own decisions and choices with informed consent.
  2. Protection – information, support and representation are provided for those in need of it.
  3. Prevention – action should be taken before harm occurs.
  4. Proportionate response – proportionality and consideration of human rights must be used in response to any risk or occurrence of abuse.
  5. Partnership and community response.
  6. Accountability and transparency.

Safeguarding Policy Statement

At Birtley House we expect our residents to live in an environment that is safe, supportive and free from abuse. Each will be able to participate in the life of the Home, without fear of any kind of abusive behaviour being directed towards them. All persons living or being cared for, working at, or visiting this home will be treated with respect for their individuality and human rights. Each person will, therefore, conduct him or her self in such a way that they can cause no offence to any other.

A culture of zero tolerance towards abuse will be maintained and the Managers will ensure that staff concerns are dealt with confidentially, thoroughly and fairly. Any abuse of power or privilege, whether perpetrated by staff, visitors or residents, will be dealt with promptly.

Abuse takes many different forms, and occurs in all cultures, all religions and all social classes. This policy is a guidance document for staff to ensure they are aware of the various types of abuse, to enable them to recognise the signs that abuse may be occurring and outlines the actions they should take if they suspect that abuse has occurred or may occur.

The organisation is committed to the aims of adult safeguarding:

  • prevent harm and reduce the risk of abuse or neglect to adults with care and support needs;
  • stop abuse or neglect wherever possible;
  • safeguard adults in a way that supports them in making choices and having control about how they want to live;
  • promote an approach that concentrates on improving life for the adults concerned;
  • raise public awareness so that communities as a whole, alongside professionals, play their part in preventing, identifying and responding to abuse and neglect;
  • provide information and support in accessible ways to help people understand the different types of abuse, how to stay safe and what to do to raise a concern about the safety or well-being of an adult;
  • address what has caused the abuse or neglect.

To contribute to meeting these aims, we will:

  • Manage our services in a way which minimises the risk of abuse occurring;
  • Work with adults with care and support needs and other agencies to end any abuse that is taking place.

To achieve these aims we will:

  • Ensure that all managers, employees and volunteers have access to and are familiar with this safeguarding adult policy and procedure and their responsibilities within it;
  • Ensure concerns or allegations of abuse are always taken seriously;
  • Ensure the Mental Capacity Act is used to make decisions on behalf of those adults at risk who are unable to make particular decisions forthemselves;
  • Ensure all staff receive training in relation safeguarding adults at a level relevant to their role;
  • Ensure that people using our services, and where relevant their relatives and their friends, have access to information about how to report concerns or allegations of abuse;
  • Ensure there is a named lead person to promote adult safeguarding awareness and practice within the organisation This policy and procedure has been developed to be consistent with the Surrey Safeguarding Adults Board Adult Safeguarding Policy and Procedures.

The Definition of Abuse

Abuse is a violation of an individual’s human and civil right by any other person or persons.

Incidents of abuse may be multiple, either to one person in a continuing relationship or service context or to more than one person at a time. Some instances of abuse will constitute a criminal offence. Criminal charges can be brought against the abuser under the Offences Against the Person Act 1861. In addition, statutory offences have been created which specifically protect those who may be incapacitated in various ways. Examples of actions which may constitute criminal offences are assault, whether physical or psychological, sexual assault and rape, theft, fraud, or other forms of exploitation, and certain forms of discrimination, whether on basis of race or gender.

Registered Nurses are bound by the NMC Code of Professional Conduct; standards for conduct, performance and ethics (2018), which states that they are responsible for ensuring that they safeguard the interests of their patients at all times.

The currently recognised forms of abuse have been broadly defined under the following categories:

  1. Physical Abuse – The non accidental infliction of physical force that results (or could result) in bodily injury pain or impairment.
    Examples of behaviour: hitting, slapping, pushing, burning, physical restraint, harassment, enforced sedation, inappropriate use of medication, and catheterisation for management ease.
  2. Sexual Abuse – Direct or indirect involvement in sexual activity without consent
    Examples include:
    • Non Contact: looking, photography, indecent exposure, harassment, serious teasing or innuendo, exposure to pornography.
    • Contact: rape, sexual assault or sexual acts to which the vulnerable adult has not consented, could not consent to, or was pressured into consenting. This includes either physical behaviour or remarks of a sexual nature, made towards the client. Examples include touching the client inappropriately or engaging in sexual discussions which have no relevance to the clients care.
  3. Neglect and Acts of Omission – Ignoring or withholding physical or medical care needs.
    Examples include: Failure to provide appropriate food, shelter, heating, clothing, medical care, hygiene personal care, appropriate medication.
  4. Psychological/Emotional Abuse – That which impinges on the emotional health and development of individuals
    Examples include shouting, swearing, threats, insulting, humiliation, intimidation, ignoring, coercion, denying the right to privacy or choice, blaming or harassment. This type of abuse is often present alongside other forms of abuse.
  5. Financial/Material Abuse - The unauthorised, fraudulent obtaining and improper use of funds, property or any resources of a vulnerable person
    Examples include borrowing property or money from a client or client’s family member, withholding client’s money or possessions and the inappropriate handling of, or accounting for, a resident’s money or possessions.
  6. Disciminatory Abuse - Abuse of individual rights is a violation of human and civil rights by any other person or persons
    Discriminatory abuse consists of abusive or derisive attitudes or behaviour based on a person’s sex, sexuality, ethnic origin, race, culture, age or disability or any other discriminatory abuse.
  7. Professional/Institutional Abuse - Professional Abuse is the misuse of therapeutic power and abuse of trust by professionals, the failure of professionals to act on suspected abuse/crimes, poor care practice or neglect in services, resource shortfalls or service pressures that lead to service failure and culpability as a result of poor management systems/structures
    Examples of behaviour; Entering into a sexual relationship with a resident, failure to refer disclosure of abuse, poor, ill-informed or outmoded care practice, failure to support vulnerable adult access to health care/treatment, denying vulnerable adult access to professional support and services such as advocacy, service design where groups of users living together are incompatible, punitive responses to challenging behaviours, failure to whistle-blow on issues when internal procedures to highlight issues are exhausted. The systems and practices in place in the home should be for the residents benefit not for the staff or the homes benefit. Choice where ever possible should be given, and person centred care plans should be used for each resident.
  8. - It is also recognised that “self-neglect” can also become an issue and should be identified and engaged with appropriately, taking in to consideration a person’s human rights and privacy concerns.

Responsibilities

Overall responsibility for safeguarding and prevention of abuse at Birtley House lies with the Registered Manager and Provider, who ensure the right level of scrutiny and oversight are given and are responsible for ensuring that all related policies and procedures are regularly reviewed and communicated to all staff and volunteers.

It is not just the responsibility of the Provider or Registered Manager to act to prevent the abuse of a vulnerable adult, it is the responsibility of every employee, volunteer, visitor, relative and resident as well.

All staff, whether paid or unpaid are expected to:

  • be aware of the Surrey Safeguarding Adults Board Policies and Procedures;
  • keep their knowledge and skills up to date by meeting the training requirements expected of their role;
  • understand what is expected of them if they become aware that an adult with care and support needs is experiencing or is at risk of abuse or neglect;
  • take all reasonable actions in line with those expectations;
  • take all reasonable actions to prevent adults with care and support needs from experiencing abuse and neglect.

Possible Signs of Abuse

The following is a list of general indicators of abuse:

  • Unexplained injuries or injuries where the description of the “accident” and the injuries do not correspond.
  • The person is quiet and withdrawn.
  • The person is nervous and eager to please.
  • Bruising, particularly to shins and forearms.
  • Withdrawal from physical contact.
  • Appears agitated and anxious, and seeks isolation.
  • Cuts and tears to skin.

This list is not exhaustive - if in even the slightest doubt, talk to a senior staff member you trust for advice on the situation. The conversation will be in the strictest confidence

Responding to a Disclosure

Any individual becoming aware of potential abuse should take action to:

  • Listen to what the vulnerable adult is saying;
  • Safeguard the vulnerable adult, alerting emergency services if necessary;
  • Record the words of the vulnerable adult and accepting the statements as truth, record the full details, including the time, date and location that the disclosure was made. All written notes must be made as soon as practicable and kept;
  • Discuss and negotiate with the vulnerable adult as to who will be informed and why.

The initial conversation should be regarded as a source of evidence. It is therefore important to listen and not ask leading questions which may suggest or invite an anticipated answer, and to record the concerns precisely, as expressed by the vulnerable adult and referrer. This initial conversation may become the basis for a formal interview at a later date.

The appropriate authorities to respond to referrals regarding the possible abuse of a vulnerable adult will be social services and the police. If there are concerns a referral should be made direct to social services or to the police if a criminal offence seems to have been committed. [[Surrey Multi Agency Safeguarding Hub (MASH)]]

Responding to a Concern or a Report of Abuse

The Company will respond as follows:

  • Birtley House will investigate any occurrence or risk of abuse that it becomes aware of, whether discovered or reported internally or externally and will, where appropriate, take immediate action, including referral to an appropriate body;
  • It may be necessary to suspend staff during any investigation;
  • Inform the investigating authority of any suspensions;
  • Follow appropriate disciplinary procedure, as a result of the investigation findings;
  • Ensure that a plan of care is made, where appropriate, in discussion with doctor and senior care staff, and implement same;
  • Ensure that action plan is monitored and that a date is set to review progress;
  • Ensure all details are carefully documented whatever the outcome and that all interested parties are informed;
  • Where required, carry out a serious case review and follow up with corrective action and changes to practice and/or implementation of any recommendations. This may involve seeking specialist advice or support.

Human Rights & Mental Capacity Act (MCA) Policy

The aim of this policy is to ensure that this organisation fully implements the Mental Capacity Act 2005 which sets out how service users who lack capacity should be helped to take decisions and under what circumstances decisions might be taken on their behalf when they lack the ability to do so.

Staff should be aware that the Act introduced a criminal offence of ill treatment or neglect of a person who lacks capacity. Any person found guilty of such an offence may be liable to imprisonment for a term of up to five years. Organisations may also be liable to prosecution under corporate manslaughter provisions if the ill treatment or neglect resulted in or contributed to the death of a person.

Certain groups of people are legally required to have regard to the Act and its associated Code of Practice when making decisions on behalf of people who lack mental capacity. This includes doctors, nurses, health and social care managers and staff. The Act applies only to people aged 16 years and over.

Background

The Mental Capacity Act 2005 was introduced into England and Wales in April 2007 and fully implemented in October 2007 when a Code of Practice was issued. It has great relevance for the provision of health and social care and in protecting the human rights of service users, particularly protecting them from abuse and improper treatment or care.

The Mental Capacity Act, or MCA as it is often known, was introduced to provide a statutory framework to protect those who lack the mental capacity to make their own decisions, such as those with severe dementia or any other significant brain dysfunction, and those who are dying and no longer capable of making decisions for themselves.

Most importantly the MCA sets out:

  1. who can take decisions for people who lack capacity;
  2. in which situations this can be done;
  3. how they should go about this.

Definition of 'Capacity'

The ‘capacity’ referred to in the MCA refers to the mental capacity to make a decision. Where a person is described as ‘lacking capacity’ this means that the person is judged as being unable to make a decision for him or herself because of an impairment of, or a disturbance in the functioning of the mind or brain, whether temporary or permanent.

Importantly, capacity can change and may relate to different decisions differently. Therefore a lack of capacity should never be assumed or presumed.

A person’s capacity to make a decision can be affected by a range of factors such as a stroke, dementia, a learning disability or a mental illness – or when they are near end of life. A person’s capacity may also vary over time or according to the type of decision to be made. For example, making decisions about what to eat will demand less mental capacity than making decisions about whether or not to have life-saving surgery.

Physical conditions, such as an intimidating or unfamiliar environment, can also affect capacity, temporarily preventing people from being able to make decisions, as can trauma, loss, medication and health problems. Lack of capacity may therefore not be a permanent condition. Assessments of capacity should be time and decision specific. It cannot be assumed that someone lacks capacity based upon age, appearance, condition or behaviour alone.

Key Principles of the Act

According to the following principles, which are legally enforceable in law, all staff involved in the care and treatment of a person who may lack capacity must show that they understand the rights of the person under the law and that they have assessed the person’s capacity appropriately. It is particularly important that they understand that a person’s capacity may change over time depending upon the decision to be made and the individual circumstances.

The Act is underpinned by five key principles set out in Section 1 of the Act:

  1. A presumption of capacity - every adult has the right to make his or her own decisions and must be assumed to have capacity to do so unless it is proved otherwise.
  2. The right for individuals to be supported to make their own decisions - people must be given all appropriate help to enable them to make their own decisions before anyone concludes that they cannot do so.
  3. Individuals must retain the right to make what might be seen as eccentric or unwise decisions – it is human nature that people sometimes make poor decisions but that does not mean that they should be judged as unable to take those decisions.
  4. The principle of ‘best interests’ – anything done for or on behalf of people without capacity must be in their best interests, taking in to account the person’s past and present wishes and feelings.
  5. The least restrictive intervention principle – anything done for or on behalf of people without capacity should be the least restrictive of their basic rights and freedoms.

Care Quality Commission Compliance

To maintain its registration with the CQC Birtley House must comply with the Health and Social Care Act 2008 (Regulated Activities) Regulations 2010, Health and Social Care Act 2008 (Regulated Activities) Regulations 2014, Health and Social Care Act 2008 (Regulated Activities) (Amendment) Regulations 2015 which contain references to the Mental Capacity Act and the need to protect the human rights of people who lack capacity, including:

  • Regulation 9: Person Centred Care, states that, where a service user lacks mental capacity to make specific decisions relating to their care and treatment, and no lawful representative has been appointed, their best interests should be established in accordance with the Mental Capacity Act 2005, and these should be acted upon;
  • Regulation 11: Need for consent, requires that care and treatment is only provided with the consent of the relevant person;
  • Regulation 12: Safe care and treatment;
  • Regulation 13: Safeguarding service users from abuse and improper treatment;
  • Regulation 20: Duty of candour (see our separate Duty of Candour Policy).

The correct application of the MCA has been included in guidance for inspectors in determining whether services are safe and effective. In particular, inspectors are prompted during visits to ask questions about:

  • whether staff understand the relevant requirements of the Mental Capacity Act 2005;
  • how and when a person’s mental capacity to consent to care or treatment is assessed and, where appropriate, recorded;
  • whether staff make best interest decisions in line with the legislation;
  • what arrangements there are to make sure that decisions about the use of restraint are made appropriately and recorded.

Assessment of Capacity

An NHS Mental Capacity Act and Deprivation of Liberty Pathway diagram follows the process of assessing capacity.

Staff should be aware of Section 2 of the Act which makes it clear that a lack of capacity cannot be established merely by reference to the person’s age, appearance, or any condition or aspect of a person’s behaviour which might lead others to make unjustified assumptions about capacity. Most people will be able to make most decisions, no matter what their age is and even when they have a label or diagnosis that may seem to imply that they do not have mental capacity, such as dementia.

Further guidance on the application of the MCA has been published by CQC and Social Care Institure for Excellence (SCIE)

The Capacity Tests

The Act states that any competent person who is aware of the requirements of the MCA can apply the test, they do not have to be a doctor (though professional opinion may be required in complex or major decision cases) or a nurse. The test is Decision-Specific which means that it must be applied for each specific decision. It should never be decided that someone lacks capacity as a result of a particular medical condition or diagnosis and therefore need never be asked. Where used the test should be properly recorded in the service user’s notes.

Staff assessing capacity must remember that an unwise or unpopular decision made by the person does not of itself indicate a lack of capacity.

A person can put his or her wishes and feelings into a written statement if they so wish, which the person making the determination must consider.

If there is a chance that the person will have capacity in the future, the decision should, if at all possible, be delayed until they do.

Staff should use the 2-stage test set out in the Act for assessing whether, on the balance of probabilities (i.e. it is more likely than not) a person lacks capacity to take a particular decision at a particular time. The 2-stage test asks:

  1. Is there an impairment of, or disturbance in, the functioning of the person’s mind or brain? If so,
  2. Is the impairment or disturbance sufficient that the person lacks the capacity to make that particular decision?

The MCA says that a person is unable to make their own decision if they cannot do one or more of the following four things (the 4-stage test):

  1. understand information given to them;

    2. OR
  2. retain the information long enough to be able to make the decision;

    3. OR
  3. weight up the information available to make the decision;

    4. OR
  4. communicate their decision (in any way, including sign language, blinking, squeezing a hand or muscle movement).

Recording the Capacity Assessment

A clearly recorded assessment of capacity, with supporting evidence should be kept, where someone is deemed to lack capacity to consent to their care and support plan, including:

  • The decision;
  • how the person was assisted to understand the decision;
  • the impairment;
  • how this affects the person’s capacity;
  • whether the decision can be delayed;
  • a reasonable belief (i.e. more likely than not) that the person lacks capacity to make the decision;
  • any referral to the commissioning body if there is a dispute.

Staff should be aware of circumstances in which it is necessary to involve external professionals and agencies, ensuring written records of assessments and decisions are kept and confirmation by the professional is obtained where they are appropriately involved in assessments and decisions.

Assessments of Day-to-Day Decisions

Where assessments of capacity relate to day–to-day decisions and caring actions, such as helping someone to wash or get out of bed, no formal assessment procedures or documentation is required. However if a decision is challenged the member of staff must be able to describe why they had a reasonable belief of lack of capacity. Anyone making a decision in the best interests of a person who lacks capacity is specifically warned in the Act not to make assumptions that cannot be clearly justified.

Assessments of Highly Significant Decisions

Where the decision is about highly significant matters, such as financial, medical or end of life matters, the assessment process has to be clear and accountable and requires input from staff across the range of organisations involved in providing support for a person. In such cases it should include family and carers and, where there is no family or carer, an Independent Mental Capacity Advocate (IMCA) may be assigned where appropriate.

Restraint & Restrictions

Restraint should only ever be used in compliance with the Act as a last resort or in exceptional circumstances. It is defined in the Act as the use or threat of force where an incapacitated person resists, and any restriction of liberty or movement, whether or not the person resists. It is only permitted if the person using it reasonably believes it is necessary to prevent harm to the person who lacks capacity, and if the restraint used is proportionate to the likelihood and seriousness of the harm.

Advance Decisions

Staff should be aware of how the Act relates to Advance Decisions, decisions made by a person in case there comes a time when they no longer have the capacity to make a decision. In such cases the Advance Decision, where properly made, should be acted upon. Further guidance is set out in Birtley House’s policies on end of life care and on advance decisions.

Independent Mental Capacity Advocate (IMCA)

Staff should be aware that in situations where decisions are being made about serious medical treatment for a person who lacks capacity and has no one to speak for them, such as family or friends, the Act makes provision for them to be supported by an Independent Mental Capacity Advocate (IMCA). The IMCA makes representations about the person’s wishes, feelings, beliefs and values, at the same time as bringing to the attention of the decision-maker all factors that are relevant to the decision. The IMCA can challenge the decision-maker on behalf of the person lacking capacity if necessary.

Lasting Power of Attorney

Staff should be aware that the Act allows a person to appoint someone with Lasting Powers of Attorney to act on their behalf if they lose capacity in the future. An attorney must be over 18 and can be a family member, friend, care worker or professional. They are always subject to the provisions of the Act, particularly the core principles and the best interests requirements.

Deprivation of Liberty

If a situation arises where a resident requires care, treatment or some form of intervention but they cannot make a decision about this because of a lack of mental capacity, but it is felt in their best interests to proceed with it, we first try to ascertain if it would lead to the person being deprived of their liberty as a result. In these circumstances, please see Policy on Deprivation of Liberty

Deprivation of Liberty Policy

From 1 April 2009 any care home or hospital treating or caring for a person in such a way that they are depriving that person of their liberty is required to seek authority to do so. This document has been written to explain the process that must be followed in order to obtain authorisation if there is not already authority given by the court.

Deprivation of Liberty is an evolving area with the current processes due to replaced in the near future. The CQC 2017/18 State of Care report covers what good practice in applying DoLS looks like – it emphasises regular training and the importance of the registered manager and staff having a good knowledge of DoLS, the MCA and fluctuating capacity. It gives examples of homes where the DoLS authorisation had been removed as capacity improved and the resident regained the ability to make relevant decisions.

Scope

This policy is written in accordance with the regulatory framework provided by the Mental Capacity Act 2005, the Deprivation of Liberty Safeguards Code of Practice 2009 and the Mental Capacity Act Code of Practice. This policy should be read with the Company's Human Rights & Mental Capacity Act (MCA) Policy.

In particular, this policy aims to ensure the Five Statutory Principles are properly implemented:

  1. A person must be assumed to have capacity unless it is established they lack capacity.
  2. A person is not to be treated as unable to make a decision unless all practicable steps to help them to do so have been taken without success.
  3. A person is not to be treated as unable to make a decision merely because he makes an unwise decision.
  4. An act done or decision made, under the Act for or on behalf of a person who lacks capacity must be done, or made in, the person’s best interests.
  5. Before the act is done, or the decision is made, regard must be had to whether the purpose for which it is needed can be as effectively achieved in a way that is less restrictive of the person’s rights and freedom of action.

Admission

When a Relevant Person is referred to the home, the Registered Manager will ensure that the referrer identifies any possibility that there may be a need for a deprivation of liberty authorisation. This authorisation should be applied for 28 days before the deprivation of liberty is due to start. In the case of an admission from the social service or NHS, they will normally inform you well before admission, however, when family arranges a placement or others it is likely that the Registered Manager will need to raise the issue.

The Registered Manager will ensure that the potential Relevant Person has their mental capacity to agree to residing at the home assessed. If they lack capacity to consent to the admission then the Registered Manager will require the referrer to supply information about the following:

  1. Does the Relevant Person have anyone with Lasting Power of Attorney?
  2. If so, is the LPA for Health and Personal Welfare issues?
  3. Does the person have a deputy appointed by the Court of Protection?
  4. If so, is the Deputy empowered for Health and Personal Welfare issues?
  5. Has the person made any Advance Decisions to Refuse Medical Treatment, which apply to anything, which may happen while they are in the home?
  6. Does the referrer know of any wishes and feelings expressed by the Relevant Person, which relate to their care? (These are sometimes called advance statements).
  7. Do they have a list of people who should be consulted when a major decision is being made?

If it is determined that the pre-admission assessment does not indicate a deprivation of liberty authorisation is necessary but circumstances change on admission then the Registered Manager will follow the urgent authorisation procedure.

Identifying if authorisation is needed

The Registered Manager will ensure that all residents are assessed for a need for authorisation. This will normally be pre-admission but on occasions will be post-admission. Sometimes a person will be admitted with no need for authorisation but because of changes in their condition the need develops over time. The home’s ongoing assessments will recognise this before deprivation is actually necessary and the Registered Manager will apply to the supervisory body for authorisation.

Determining a Deprivation of Liberty

The basis for determining whether a person is deprived of liberty is subject to an evolving legal position. The starting point should be the specific facts of the Relevant Person’s situation. A range of factors should be considered, including the type, duration, manner and implementation of the measure in question. Whether there has been a deprivation of liberty will then be a question of degree and intensity. The “Acid Test” set out by the Supreme Court in Cheshire West, when assessing if the Relevant Person is deprived of liberty, is whether that person is:

  1. Under continuous or complete supervision and control;
  2. Not free to leave;
  3. Lacks capacity to consent to these arrangements.

Urgent Life-saving Medical Treatment

This policy also takes in to consideration the recent guidance resulting from the Ferreira court of appeal judgement, of which staff will be made aware. In light of this guidance, authorisation will not be sought for depriving the Relevant Person of their liberty where the need to do so arises in the context of urgent life-saving medical treatment, in accordance with regulation 17 Health and Social Care Act 2008 (Regulated Activities) Regulations 2014. Such circumstances would arise where:

  1. The relevant Person lacked capacity to make decisions about their own care as a consequence of their physical health condition (for example, they are unconscious);

    2. AND
  2. The patient would have been treated the same way irrespective of whether they had the capacity to make their own decisions when conscious;

    3. AND
  3. If the patient were conscious and chose to leave, they would not be prevented from doing so.

Indicators of a potential Deprivation of Liberty

If the Registered Manager believes that the arrangements for care MIGHT represent deprivation of liberty, they must apply for authorisation.

Points, which MIGHT indicate Deprivation of Liberty, include:

  1. • Restraint or sedation on admission where the resident resists.
  2. Complete and effective control over care and movement of the person.
  3. Control over assessments, treatment, contacts and residence.
  4. A decision has been made that the person will not be released to the care of others, or permitted to live elsewhere, unless the staff consider it appropriate.
  5. There has been a refusal to let the person live with a relative.
  6. The person’s social contacts are determined for them, e.g. they are not allowed contact with particular people including relatives.
  7. Ongoing restraint or sedation required.
  8. The person receives sedative medication for which they do not have capacity to consent.
  9. The person is restricted to a small part of the home.
  10. The person receives high level of observation.
  11. The person seems to be trying to leave.

Best Interests Decisions

The balance of these issues and others will determine deprivation of liberty. If none of these apply, then it is unlikely that there is a deprivation of liberty. It is a requirement that where a deprivation of liberty is authorised it must be the least restrictive option that is proportionate to the need to prevent harm and is in the Relevant Person’s best interest.

The best interests of a person subject to a deprivation of liberty will be considered in accordance with the Code of Practice and the following guidelines:

  1. All relevant circumstances should be taken in to account.
  2. Decisions should not be made on the basis of a person’s age or appearance alone, as this may lead to unjustified assumptions.
  3. If there is a chance that the person will have capacity in the future, the decision should, if at all possible, be delayed until they do.
  4. The person should be encouraged and helped to join in making the decision when possible.
  5. If the decision is about life sustaining medical treatment, it must not be motivated by a wish to hasten a person’s death.
  6. The person’s past and present wishes, feelings, beliefs and values must be considered.
  7. The views of other relevant people should be considered, in particular:
    • Anyone the person has asked to be consulted.
    • Those involved in caring for the person.
    • Anyone else interested in their welfare.
    • An IMCA.
    • Holders of a Lasting Power of Attorney.
    • Any court appointed deputy.
    • There should be no discrimination.

It is not uncommon for friends and family to have different views about the treatment or care of a loved one. Disputes may be resolved through getting a second opinion, mediation or referring to the Court of Protection for a ruling.

All discussions and decisions in relation to best interest decision should be recorded in the Care Plan.

Independent Mental Capacity Advocate (IMCA)

An IMCA is an “Independent Mental Capacity Advocate”. Whenever someone who lacks mental capacity to consent to arrangements for their accommodation has no family or unpaid carers, it is necessary that an IMCA be involved to oversee the decision-making process. When applying for authorization, the Managing Authority must inform the Supervisory Body if an IMCA is or should be involved.

The Relevant Person’s Representative may also have a right to access an IMCA.

Supervisory Body

The supervisory body will be the local social services authority (LSSA) arranging, paying for, or contributing to the Relevant Person’s care.

Where the Relevant Person is of no fixed abode it will be the local LSSA who will deal with this home.

For self/privately funded Relevant Person’s it will be the local LSSA who will deal with this home.

If it is difficult to ascertain where the Relevant Person lives, then the Registered Manager will apply for authorisation to the local LSSA.

Where the Relevant Person care is paid for by the health authority then the Supervisory Body will be the LSSA associated with the health authority.

The Registered Manager is responsible for keeping records of which LSSA is paying for the Relevant Person’s care to ensure that an authorisation request is sent to the appropriate Supervisory Body.

Assessment

Reference could be made to the NHS's Mental Capacity Act and Deprivation of Liberty Pathway

The Supervisory Body carries out or arranges the assessment and the role of the Managing Authority is to facilitate the assessments. The assessment is in six parts, and must be completed within 21 days, for a standard authorization.

Members of staff will cooperate with the assessments carried out in respect of the deprivation of liberty authorisation process. The assessment is vital to protection the rights of the Relevant Person and staff members should do all they can to facilitate the process.

The assessors will need to see information held about the Relevant Person to help them come to a decision. This information might include:

  • Evidence of age.
  • Information about their daily care and treatment.
  • Care plans and risk assessments.
  • Information about illnesses.
  • Contact information for family and others.
  • Forms relating to legal issues, such as the Act.
  • Evidence to show that deprivation is the least restrictive option.
  • Any information regarding mental capacity such as:
    • Any notes about what the Relevant Person may have said about wishes, views and feelings;
    • Any information about people with a Lasting Power of Attorney for the Relevant Person;
    • Any advance decisions to refuse medical treatment the Relevant Person may have on their file;
    • Other information they consider relevant.

It is possible that an assessment may take place outside normal working hours and staff on duty should be made aware of this.

During the assessment, however, staff still have a duty of care to the Relevant Person. They should raise concerns with the assessor if they sense the Relevant Person being assessed, whom they know better than the assessor, is becoming distressed.

The time from the point at which the Supervisory Body accepts the referral to the issuing of a standard authorisation should not be more than 21 days.

When an urgent authorisation has been granted to the home, the assessment has to be completed within 7 days. Under special circumstances the Supervisory Body can extend this by a further 7 days.

To apply for a standard authorisation the Registered Manager must complete Form 1 – standard and urgent request.

It is vital that the whole form is completed or the assessment will be delayed or prolonged. The form is to be sent to the Supervisory Body for the Relevant Person or potential Relevant Person.

The assessors will submit their reports to the Supervisory Body and an authorisation will be issued. Authorisation can be given for any period up to 1 year. In unstable situations it is likely that the period will be considerably less than one year.

The authorisation is an important legal document and must be filed as part of the Relevant Person’s records. The authorisation may include conditions to which the managing authority and all staff must adhere to make the Deprivation of Liberty legal. When authorisation is received, the Registered Manager is responsible for instructing all staff that will have contact with the Relevant Person in any conditions, which apply.

This briefing process will be recorded within the Relevant Person’s record. All staff members present at the briefing are to sign the record to provide evidence that the briefing has taken place and they fully understand what is expected of them.

Review

The Registered Manager (Managing Authority) has an ongoing duty to review whether or not a Deprivation of Liberty Authorisation is still required.

There are three circumstances under which this should be done:

  • When there are any changes in a Relevant Person’s condition or arrangements which may indicate a change in their need for deprivation of liberty;

    • OR
  • When a period of authorised deprivation is coming to an end;

    • OR
  • If the Registered Manager (Managing Authority) is not certain about the need for continued deprivation of liberty.

As soon as the Registered Manager (Managing Authority) thinks that they can look after the Relevant Person safely without the need to deprive them of their liberty, they should do so immediately.

All decisions in relation to a review should be recorded in the Care Plan.

A request for a review will be made to the Supervisory Body using Form 10 – review request.

Urgent Authorisation

There will be occasions when it is discovered that a person who is already admitted is living under circumstances, which may indicate that they are being deprived of their liberty without a valid authorisation. As soon as this is suspected it is the legal duty of the Registered Manager (Managing Authority) to grant themselves an Urgent authorisation. If there is no authorisation the deprivation would be illegal and the Registered Manager (Managing Authority) would be liable for prosecution. It is essential therefore that action is taken the instant that deprivation of liberty is suspected.

To grant an Urgent Authorisation the Registered Manager (Managing Authority) must:

  • Complete Form 1 – standard and urgent request;

If the Registered Manager (Managing Authority) is not available to complete and sign the forms, it will be done by one of his/her delegated representatives, the list of these delegated staff is held by the most senior person on duty.

The Urgent Authorisation lasts for 7 days. Day one starts the day the authorisation is signed and dated. The Supervisory Body may extend this by a further 7 days in exceptional circumstances. To apply for an extension Form 2 - further authorisation request must be completed by the Registered Manager (Managing Authority) or their delegated representative, only if they are unavailable.

Short-Term Suspension of Authorisation

There are separate review arrangements for cases in which the eligibility requirement ceases to be met for a short period of time. For example, if the Relevant Person is detained as a hospital in-patient under the Mental Health Act 1983, the managing authority must notify the supervisory body, who will suspend the authorisation – Form 7 - suspension of standard authorisation is be used.

If the Relevant Person then becomes eligible again within 28 days, the managing authority must notify the supervisory body who will remove the suspension. If no such notice is given within 28 days, then the authorisation will be terminated – Form 7 - suspension of standard authorisation is be used.

Relevant Person

The Registered Manager (Managing Authority) has a legal duty to:

  • involve the Relevant Person in every aspect of their care and treatment in accordance with the MCA.
  • inform the Relevant Person if they intend to make a request for an authorisation;
  • inform the Relevant Person when an authorisation has been granted;

    • OR
  • give information on deprivation of liberty safeguards to the Relevant Person;
  • explain their rights under DOLS, orally and in writing – as per Representative below

The Supervisory Body has a legal duty to:

  • give the Relevant Person a copy of the standard authorisation;

    • OR
  • a copy of the refusal;
  • appoint the Relevant Person’s representative;
  • appoint the Relevant Person’s IMCA.

Relevant Person's Representative (RPR)

The Supervisory Body will appoint a representative for every person subject to a Deprivation of Liberty authorisation. This person will be independent of the commissioners and providers of their service.

The Registered Manager (Managing Authority) must, as soon as possible after authorisation, ensure that the Relevant Person and their representative understand:

  • the effect of the authorisation;
  • their right to request a review;
  • the formal and informal complaints procedures available to them;
  • their right to apply to the Court of Protection to seek variation or termination of the authorisation;
  • their right to an IMCA if the representative is not a paid professional representative.

The Registered Manager (Managing Authority) is responsible for informing the representative of any sources of relevant information and support.

The Registered Manager will keep a record of the amount of contact between the representative and Relevant Person. If there is not enough contact between the representative and the Relevant Person, the Registered Manager must inform the Supervisory Body.

The Registered Manager (Managing Authority) should involve the representative when reviewing the care of the Relevant Person.

Monitoring & Audit

The managing authority will keep a record of all authorization requests and related information, statistics and details. This data should be made available to the Care Quality Commission and Department of Health if requested.

Refusal of Authorisation

As soon as the assessors decide that authorisation is not appropriate, the assessments will end.

If authorisation is not given because it is judged that no deprivation of liberty is, or will be, taking place, the current arrangements will continue.

If the reason is that although deprivation of liberty is happening, one or more of the criteria are not met, action will have to be taken immediately to make the care plan legal. The action needed will depend on the circumstances and the reason the authorisation was refused. These circumstances are laid out in Appendix A: Failed Assessment Table

The NHS has produced a Deprivation of Liberty Pathway for making an assessment, and a Deprivation Checklist to be used when a person does not have capacity to consent to admission and/or care plan, and when there is a change of circumstances.
